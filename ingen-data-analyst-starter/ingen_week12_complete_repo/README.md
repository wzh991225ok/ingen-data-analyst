## Findings
- **Parts & dispatch is 53% of all process time — and 100% of it is waiting.** Nobody is working during it, so headcount cannot shorten it. Top 3 stages = 90% of process time.
- The drivers in the brief (product, severity, geography, weekday, workload) explain **4%** of cycle-time variance. One structural fact — did the ticket need a part? — takes R² to **72%** (+578% [+555%, +601%]).
- **Regional parts buffer: −23%** mean cycle time, P90 64h→47h. **+1 Field Ops FTE: −8%**. **Both: −29%.**
- **Rerouting Tier-1 triage does nothing measurable** (−1.9%, CI spans zero) — triage is only 4% of process time. Amdahl's law: +1 Field FTE nearly zeroes field-queue wait (4.9h→0.4h) but buys only 8%, because that stage is ~15% of the process.
- The process is broadly **in statistical control** — long cycle times are what this process delivers by design, not anomalies to firefight.

## Method
Week 7's `fact_support_tickets` has no stages, teams or workload, so the lifecycle layer is generated by a
documented discrete-event model (simpy) that **replays Week 7's real ticket stream**. Reconciliation is enforced
and tested: 1:1 ticket_key join, and stage durations sum exactly to each ticket's cycle time.

## Data integrity
Every number is a property of the documented model, **not a measurement of InGen's support org**. Team sizes were
chosen to load the teams realistically against the observed ~6.8 tickets/day. What transfers is the **validated
method + reusable pipeline**: the driver regression recovered 7/9 known generating-effect directions (the 2 misses
are small, non-significant product effects diluted below noise). No shift calendar is modelled, so the null weekday
result is not evidence that weekday doesn't matter in reality. Real ticket data with stage timestamps would turn
this from a validated method into an actual answer.
